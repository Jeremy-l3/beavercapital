The Beaver Law of Capital

Exploring why monetary systems naturally evolve toward plutocracy, using an ecological metaphor.

The Core Hypothesis

Plutocracy isn't just an accident of greed. It's a structural consequence of any system where a circulating resource, like capital, pools unevenly. This concentration creates leverage points favoring "keystone actors" who, like beavers in an ecosystem, reshape their environment. They engineer new forms of shared life but also create asymmetric power structures. This is the "Beaver Law" of capital.

Capital Pools Like Water

Just as water flows and collects in basins, capital accumulates where exchange and productivity create surpluses. Over time, this natural tendency leads to significant concentration of wealth.

The Impact of Keystone Actors

In monetary systems, keystone actors are not just wealthy individuals; they are often large corporations or financial institutions that exert immense influence. Their actions reshape the economic landscape, creating both opportunities and significant power imbalances. This influence extends beyond the market into the political sphere.

Feedback Loops: Stability vs. Collapse

Healthy ecosystems have natural checks and balances. In monetary systems, these feedback loops must be designed. Unchecked capital accumulation increases systemic risk, while mechanisms like progressive taxation can act as stabilizing forces.

The Risk of Unchecked Accumulation

Studies show a strong correlation between rising wealth inequality and the probability of financial crises. As the wealth share of the top 1% increases, it can fuel asset bubbles and create systemic vulnerabilities.

A 1 standard deviation rise in the growth of the top 1% wealth share is associated with a 3 to 8 percentage point increase in financial crisis probability.
